# Design Rationale

I designed the tree as one conversation with three sequential shifts: agency, contribution, and radius. The structure matters as much as the wording. Axis 1 asks the employee to separate circumstances from response. Axis 2 then builds on that by asking what they brought to the day, not what they received from it. Axis 3 widens the frame further by asking who else sits inside their concern. This order mirrors the assignment's intended progression: people first see their own agency, then their contribution, then their connection to something beyond themselves.

I limited the runtime system to deterministic components only: fixed options, explicit routing rules, simple signal tallies, and string interpolation. There is no free text, no scoring model, and no LLM call. That constraint shaped the design. I avoided options that would require interpretation after the answer. Each option is already semantically mapped to one side of an axis, so the runtime only performs lookups. I also kept the reflections short and non-moralizing. The goal is not to "grade" the employee. The goal is to help them notice a pattern without shame.

The questions were chosen to surface behavior rather than self-image. Instead of asking "Are you proactive?" or "Do you care about others?", the tree asks about a concrete day, a concrete interaction, and a concrete payoff that matters. That choice reduces socially desirable responding. I also avoided overly polished options that no tired employee would pick at 7pm. For example, "I kept score of who was doing more work" is more realistic than an abstract entitlement statement. The tree should feel like a wise colleague, not a psychometric test.

My main psychological anchors were the ones named in the brief: Rotter on locus of control, Dweck on growth-oriented agency, Campbell et al. on entitlement, Organ on citizenship behavior, Maslow on self-transcendence, and Batson on perspective-taking. I used those as conceptual boundaries, not as labels shown to the employee. The product should encode the insight, not lecture the user about the theory.

I used AI during design, but I treated it as a drafting and critique tool, not a source of truth. The guardrails were: ask for multiple alternative phrasings, reject options that felt too clean or too leading, avoid unsupported psychological claims, and convert every fuzzy suggestion into an explicit deterministic branch before keeping it. I also used negative prompting such as: "Do not give me therapy language, do not invent evidence, do not use free-text classification, and do not output options that require later interpretation." Where I disagreed with AI most often was tone. Drafts tended to become preachy, diagnostic, or too inspirational. I cut those back and kept the reflections plain.

With more time, I would improve three things. First, I would test the option wording with 8 to 10 real users and trim any option pairs that feel too close together. Second, I would add a small tie-break layer for Axis 3 so the system can distinguish a team-oriented radius from a customer-oriented radius. Third, I would add a manager-facing analytics view that aggregates axis trends over time without exposing individual reflections in a punitive way.
